Introduction to the Evolving Paradigm of Autonomous
Aerial Systems
The deployment of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) for autonomous delivery operations
represents one of the most critical logistical and computational advancements of the
contemporary era. Operating these complex cyber-physical systems within dynamic,
unstructured, and entirely unmapped environments necessitates a profound departure from
traditional, deterministic computational models. The fundamental challenge lies in enabling
multi-drone networks to perform real-time, collision-free trajectory planning while
simultaneously maintaining secure, decentralized, and trustworthy communication in the
presence of malicious nodes, signal degradation, and severe environmental uncertainty. As the
utilization of autonomous drones expands across last-mile logistics, precision agriculture,
urban firefighting, and disaster response, the imperative for robust, autonomous, and secure
systems has reached a critical zenith.
The thesis under exhaustive examination proposes a highly integrated, multi-layered
architecture designed specifically to resolve these compounding and often conflicting
challenges. By synthesizing state-of-the-art continuous Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL)
algorithms to achieve sequential decision-making autonomy, and an ultra-lightweight
Blockchain topology to facilitate secure, isolated, and decentralized communication among
multi-agent swarms, the proposed framework attempts to bridge the historically disparate
domains of individual kinematic intelligence and network-level operational security.
Furthermore, the project methodology leverages the modern Qualcomm Flight RB5 and
Robotics RB6 platforms, utilizing the Gazebo physics simulator and ROS 2 middleware to
simulate highly realistic 3D physical environments, employing complex spatial data structures
such as segment trees to manage high-dimensional 3D coordinate arrays, and interactive
midpoint-to-endpoint trajectory calculations to optimize flight paths.
This report provides an exhaustive, peer-level analysis of the proposed thesis statement. It
contextualizes the core concepts within the absolute state-of-the-art research landscape
spanning the years 2024 through 2026. By meticulously deconstructing the theoretical

mechanisms underpinning the DRL algorithms, the six-layer blockchain integration, and the

Theoretical Deconstruction of the Thesis Architecture
The thesis delineates a sophisticated, multi-phased implementation strategy aimed at
achieving highly robust drone delivery operations capable of functioning seamlessly in both
known and unknown environments. The implementation spans the preparation of the training
environment, the development of interactive planning and foundational blockchain
communications, the integration of these systems for secure data handling, and finally, the
rigorous training and testing in realistic 3D simulations. To accurately ascertain the viability of
this approach, it is imperative to dissect the constituent technological pillars that form the
foundation of the proposed system.

Sequential Decision-Making via Deep Reinforcement Learning
Traditional path-planning methodologies, such as A* search algorithms, Dijkstra's algorithm,
Rapidly-exploring Random Trees (RRT), and Artificial Potential Fields (APF), rely heavily on
predefined environmental maps, static occupancy grids, and deterministic heuristic functions.
While these classical optimization methods are computationally efficient and mathematically
provable in static, known environments, they exhibit catastrophic performance degradation in
unknown or highly dynamic environments where obstacle topologies shift unpredictably or
where fast-moving targets are present. The thesis explicitly and correctly addresses this
profound limitation by adopting a Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) framework to navigate
the complexities of partial observability, sensor noise, and stochastic environmental dynamics.
The DRL paradigm fundamentally frames the autonomous 3D navigation challenge as a Markov
Decision Process (MDP). Within this framework, the drone acts as the artificial agent,
interacting with the 3D airspace (the environment) across discrete and continuous time steps.
At each sequential step, the agent observes a state representation derived from onboard
sensors (such as depth cameras, LiDAR point clouds, and inertial measurement units), selects
an action based on a highly parameterized policy, and receives a scalar reward signal. This
reward signal is meticulously engineered to penalize catastrophic states such as collisions,
excessive energy consumption, or boundary violations, while incentivizing proximity to the
target coordinate and smooth kinematic execution.
While early iterations of drone autonomy relied on discrete Deep Q-Networks (DQN), the
updated thesis architecture utilizes Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) to handle the
continuous action spaces required for smooth 3D aerial maneuvers. PPO has become the
industry standard in 2026 due to its stability and computational efficiency, allowing drones to

maintain stable flight and robust obstacle avoidance in cluttered spaces without the local
minima traps associated with classical methods. Additionally, the architecture incorporates the
Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) algorithm, a state-of-the-art off-policy DRL method that leverages
maximum entropy principles to achieve robust and sample-efficient policy optimization. By
balancing exploration and exploitation through entropy regularization, SAC ensures stable
learning and adaptability in complex, dynamic environments.
The project directly confronts the classic algorithmic "Exploration versus Exploitation" dilemma
inherent in all reinforcement learning applications. In the context of the thesis, exploration is
deployed to systematically gain knowledge of an entirely unknown environment, allowing the
agent to map out the spatial distribution of obstacles and safe flight corridors through trial and
error. Conversely, exploitation utilizes the consolidated knowledge embedded within the neural
network's learned weights to deterministically select the optimal sequence of continuous
aerodynamic actions that yield the safest and most efficient delivery path.

The Qualcomm Edge-AI Simulation Ecosystem and the Sim-to-Real
Transfer
Training a deep neural network to autonomously operate a highly complex, nonlinear,
underactuated system like a quadrotor in a dense, unknown environment requires an immense
volume of interaction data. The thesis accurately notes that high computational requirements
and extended training phases—often requiring upwards of 150,000 discrete episodes for full
algorithmic convergence—are primary challenges. Conducting this sheer volume of
trial-and-error training in the physical world is entirely cost-prohibitive, excessively
time-consuming, and operationally dangerous due to the inevitable hardware destruction that
occurs during the early, highly randomized exploration phases of the learning curve.
To elegantly circumvent this physical bottleneck, the thesis utilizes the state-of-the-art
Qualcomm Flight RB5 5G Platform and Robotics RB6 architecture, deeply integrated with the
modern Gazebo physics engine and ROS 2 (Robot Operating System). The Qualcomm platform
introduces a powerful heterogeneous computing environment. It utilizes the QRB5165
processor equipped with the 5th generation Qualcomm Artificial Intelligence Engine and a
dedicated Hexagon Tensor Accelerator (HTA) that delivers 15 TOPS of computational
performance at ultra-low power consumption, making it explicitly tailored for deep learning
workloads directly at the edge. Furthermore, the platform supports up to 7 concurrent
cameras and handles AI processing for up to 24 simultaneous 1080p video streams, ensuring
rich state observations.
This simulation ecosystem is constructed using a Software-in-the-Loop (SITL) configuration.
ROS 2 acts as the critical middleware, using tools like ros_gz to bridge the gap between
high-level Python DRL algorithms, which utilize the Data Distribution Service (DDS) protocol,
and Gazebo's internal physics transport messaging. Within Gazebo, the system simulates the
drone's motion, complex physical interactions, and synthetic sensor outputs (such as depth
maps from up to 7 concurrent simulated cameras), which are then streamed to the PX4 flight

controller via MAVLink. This ensures the neural network is trained on high-fidelity,
high-dimensional inputs that precisely mimic the physical hardware's perception.
Perhaps the most operationally significant aspect of the thesis's methodology is the application
of transfer learning to bridge the notoriously difficult sim-to-real gap. Neural networks trained
from scratch in physical environments require substantial computational budgets that wildly
exceed standard payload and battery capacities. By initially training the primary neural network
on a diverse set of generalized meta-environments within Gazebo, the network learns
fundamental feature extraction regarding depth perception and obstacle avoidance. Once the
DRL policies and the blockchain consensus mechanisms are validated in the SITL setup,
researchers can deploy the exact same ROS 2 codebase directly to the physical Qualcomm
RB5 edge-AI hardware without significant modification. This seamless transfer learning
paradigm drastically reduces onboard computational overhead, satisfying the stringent power
budgets characteristic of autonomous aerial logistics while maintaining high navigational
accuracy.

Decentralized Security Architecture: Blockchain in Multi-UAV Swarms
The integration of blockchain technology serves as the second foundational pillar of the thesis,
establishing it as a highly interdisciplinary endeavor. In a traditional multi-drone delivery
network, the reliance on a centralized command-and-control server or a single ground control
station introduces critical operational vulnerabilities. These include severe single points of
failure, unmanageable latency bottlenecks during swarm scaling, and extreme susceptibility to
adversarial attacks such as denial-of-service, GPS spoofing, and malicious data injection. The
thesis explicitly mitigates these existential risks by establishing a secure, isolated, and
completely decentralized blockchain network, augmented by Wi-Fi 6 and 5G connectivity
available on the Qualcomm RB5 hardware, to facilitate trustworthy communication links among
the individual units of the drone swarm.
The blockchain topology proposed in the thesis aligns perfectly with the advanced six-layer
architecture that has become the standard in distributed ledger research for Internet of Things
(IoT) and cyber-physical systems.1 An exhaustive analysis of these layers within the context of
the thesis reveals the depth of the proposed security model.

The execution of smart contracts within this six-layer architecture enables profound autonomy
in collaborative delivery missions. For instance, when an exploratory drone navigating an
unknown environment discovers a previously unmapped obstacle, the onboard sensory data is
processed via the Qualcomm Hexagon DSP, cryptographically hashed using ASCON ciphers 3,

3D Environment Representation and Spatial Data
Structures
The precise mathematical manner in which an autonomous aerial system perceives, discretizes,
and stores its spatial reality completely dictates its computational speed, memory
consumption, and operational agility. The thesis proposes representing the operational
environment as a continuous 3D array of coordinates, categorized in a binary format as either
"safe" (navigable airspace) or "unsafe" (occupancy obstacles). To manage the immense volume
of data generated by high-resolution 3D occupancy grids during high-speed flight, the
architecture employs a specialized data structure known as segment trees.

Discretization of Airspace and Segment Tree Querying
While a significant portion of contemporary 3D drone mapping utilizes probabilistic OctoMaps,
Voxel Grids, or continuous Truncated Signed Distance Fields (TSDF), the thesis's specific
utilization of segment trees provides unique, mathematically elegant advantages that are
directly synergistic with Deep Reinforcement Learning.
In theoretical computer science, a segment tree is a highly specialized hierarchical binary tree
data structure engineered to answer range queries and execute array interval modifications in
logarithmic
time complexity. When applied to a vast 3D coordinate array
representing physical airspace, the segment tree allows the autonomous DRL agent to execute
complex spatial queries with extraordinary computational efficiency. For example, instead of

iterating through millions of individual voxels to ensure a planned flight path is clear, the
algorithm can query a specific spatial segment (a 3D bounding box) and instantly determine
the minimum safety value or maximum obstacle density within that entire range.
Furthermore, segment trees are mathematically integral to the advanced reinforcement
learning training mechanisms frequently paired with deep networks. Specifically, the Prioritized
Experience Replay (PER) algorithm—which drastically accelerates the learning convergence of
a neural network by preferentially sampling training experiences that generated the highest
Temporal Difference (TD) error—relies entirely on a specific variant known as a
MinSegmentTree. The SegmentTree class allows for the highly efficient computation of the sum
of priorities over arbitrary segments of the Prioritized Replay Buffer array, while the
MinSegmentTree explicitly handles minimum operation queries to properly normalize the
importance-sampling weights during network backpropagation.4 By utilizing the exact same
underlying logic structure to manage both the physical 3D environment array and the neural
network's highly active memory buffer, the thesis introduces a memory-efficient architectural
consolidation perfectly suited for resource-constrained UAV hardware.

Interactive Planning: Midpoint and Endpoint Exploitation
The interactive trajectory planning module outlined in the thesis capitalizes heavily on this
efficient spatial data structure by utilizing localized sensor scans to identify navigable
"midpoints". The environment is processed dynamically in real-time, where continuous sensory
sweeps from onboard simulated LiDAR or depth cameras generate localized midpoints
representing the widest angular gaps or the safest immediate coordinates relative to the
drone's current position and orientation, enforcing a separation margin between the payload
midpoints and the corridor boundaries to enhance safety.5
The DRL algorithm processes these derived midpoints to iteratively interpolate continuous,
collision-free paths toward the final delivery "endpoints". This midpoint-to-endpoint
exploitation strategy mimics the localized receding-horizon control architectures found in
advanced mobile robotics. By forcing the neural network to focus on navigating securely to the
next computationally derived midpoint rather than attempting to solve the entire macroscopic
path to an endpoint situated miles away, the system prevents the deep neural network from
suffering catastrophic forgetting or falling into insurmountable local minima traps.
This methodology aligns closely with the Dynamic Window Approach (DWA) and A* smoothing
techniques utilized to ensure kinematic feasibility. It explicitly addresses the thesis's core
requirement of navigating unknown environments; the drone never rigidly commits to a
long-range trajectory that may suddenly be invalidated by moving obstacles or previously
unmapped building structures, ensuring continuous flexibility and survival.

Advancements in DRL-based Trajectory Planning and Continuous
Control
Advancing beyond foundational discrete-action networks, the revised architecture successfully
aligns with 2025-2026 standards by implementing continuous control algorithms. Proximal
Policy Optimization (PPO) and Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) have superseded discrete DQNs for
low-level flight control where smooth aerodynamics are prioritized. PPO enables drones to
maintain stable flight while simultaneously calculating complex avoidance trajectories.6
Furthermore, SAC is perfectly suited for these tasks, as it augments the standard objective of
maximizing expected cumulative rewards with an entropy regularization term, drastically
improving sample efficiency during continuous simulation training.

Multi-Agent Systems and Dec-POMDP Formulation
Recent advancements heavily emphasize the emergence of Multi-Agent Deep Reinforcement
Learning (MADRL) and the formal mathematical modeling of multi-drone swarms. The thesis's
framework directly mirrors current research resolving the Decentralized Partially Observable
Markov Decision Process (Dec-POMDP) problem.7 Dec-POMDP is the mathematical
framework modeling situations where decentralized multi-agents (the drones) operate with
only partial observability of the global environment, yet must jointly optimize routing decisions
without full communication.7 By integrating a Blockchain-based Trust Management Mechanism
(BTMM) that dynamically evaluates the trust values of individual drones based on delivery rate
and transmission path correctness, the thesis's approach directly addresses the exact
intersection of cybersecurity and swarm kinematics currently dominating high-impact 2025
publications, which have shown to reduce routing delays by up to 16.6% compared to
non-BTMM methods.8

Critical Evaluation of Thesis Novelty
A rigorous review of the provided thesis parameters against the extensive corpus of
2024–2026 robotics and blockchain literature confirms the high validity and impact of this
research.

When isolated, the individual technological components of the thesis are not entirely novel. The
theoretical application of blockchain for securing IoT devices and multi-drone communication
has been extensively explored and published across numerous MDPI and IEEE journals.
However, the true academic, industrial, and operational novelty of this thesis emerges
profoundly from the intersectional synthesis of these technologies into a single, cohesive
architecture engineered specifically to conquer the unique challenges of unknown
environments.
1.​ Coupled Kinematic and Cryptographic Autonomy: The vast majority of current
research remains heavily siloed; studies either focus obsessively on making drones fly
better using advanced DRL variants, or they focus on making drones communicate
securely using cryptographic ledgers. The thesis proposes a tightly coupled architecture
where the DRL agent's physical environmental discovery directly and securely informs the
blockchain's decentralized ledger. Navigating entirely unknown environments implies that
the physical environmental map is in a constant state of flux. Using programmable smart
contracts on the blockchain to autonomously and immutably propagate these spatial
updates across a multi-drone swarm—ensuring that trailing drones do not waste energy
re-exploring the same unsafe airspace and cannot be fed spoofed obstacle data by a
malicious actor—represents a highly novel, cutting-edge approach to secure,
decentralized swarm intelligence.
2.​ Dual-Purpose Segment Tree Utilization: Applying complex segment trees for the
dynamic 3D range querying of physical airspace while simultaneously leveraging the
mathematical properties of those exact same trees to manage the neural network's
prioritized experience replay buffer is a highly elegant, resource-efficient paradigm.4
Given the severe payload, memory, and battery constraints of commercial delivery
drones, this specific algorithmic efficiency provides significant practical value and stands
out as a unique structural optimization.
3.​ Deployable Sim-to-Real Pipeline: Many advanced DRL papers remain purely theoretical,
permanently confined to the perfect mathematical confines of simulation environments.
The thesis demonstrates a highly mature understanding of the sim-to-real gap. By
explicitly outlining the methodology of utilizing the official Qualcomm RB5/RB6 edge-AI
hardware in conjunction with a Gazebo and ROS 2 Software-in-the-Loop (SITL) simulation,
the thesis aggressively moves beyond theoretical simulation into actual, deployable
robotics engineering.

Historic Bottlenecks and How the Revised
Architecture Resolves Them
By directly addressing the weaknesses often associated with legacy drone autonomy and
cryptographic integration, the architecture establishes a superior operational benchmark:
●​ The Discretization Penalty in Continuous Control: Standard legacy DQNs suffered from
severe discretization errors, resulting in suboptimal, highly erratic flight paths that

depleted battery life. Resolution: By completely replacing the discrete model with
Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) for continuous
aerodynamic control, the system produces stable, robust, and highly agile trajectories
perfectly suited for the physical physics of a quadrotor.
●​ Blockchain Latency vs. Flight Velocity: Blockchain consensus mechanisms inherently
introduce network latency. In a high-speed drone delivery scenario, a delay of even 500
milliseconds in verifying a spatial update could result in a catastrophic collision.
Resolution: The integration of the CG-PBFT algorithm drastically reduces communication
complexity. By utilizing a reputation score evaluation mechanism and grouping consensus,
CG-PBFT achieves a 250% increase in throughput and a 70% reduction in latency
compared to traditional PBFT, making it exceptionally viable for high-speed UAV swarms.
●​ Cryptographic Energy Consumption: The energy required to continuously calculate
cryptographic hashes to append blocks to the data layer is substantial. Resolution: The
architecture explicitly integrates the ASCON family of ultra-lightweight ciphers, ensuring
that the security layer operates with minimal memory usage and computational overhead,
preserving the crucial battery reserves required for physical propulsion.3
